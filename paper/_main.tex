\title{Measuring Stakeholder Ideology: Political Alignment Across Employees and Consumers}

\author{Max Kagan}
\affil{Columbia Business School}

\date{\today}


\begin{singlespacing}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Firms increasingly navigate a polarized political landscape in which their stakeholders---employees, consumers, investors---may hold conflicting political preferences. Yet we lack systematic measurement of how stakeholder political composition varies across firms and whether alignment between stakeholder groups affects firm outcomes. This paper develops novel measures of stakeholder political ideology by linking granular foot traffic data to election results and employment records to voter registration. Using 596 million point-of-interest-month observations from Advan and 45 million individual employment records from Politics at Work, we construct the first comprehensive panel of both consumer and employee partisan composition at the firm level. Our analysis reveals substantial variation in stakeholder ideology across industries, geographies, and brands. We document the correlation between employee and consumer political composition, identifying where stakeholder groups align and where they diverge. Using SafeGraph Spend data and exploiting temporal variation in partisan conflict salience as an identification strategy, we examine whether stakeholder ideological alignment affects firm performance. Our findings extend stakeholder theory by introducing ideological heterogeneity as a critical dimension of stakeholder management and provide practical implications for firms operating in divided societies.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Stakeholder theory, political ideology, consumer behavior, employee ideology, corporate political activity
\end{abstract}
