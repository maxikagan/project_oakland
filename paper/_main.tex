\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Stakeholder theory urges firms to consider the interests of all firm stakeholders but offers limited guidance when stakeholder groups hold divergent preferences. I introduce the concept of ``stakeholder political pluralism'' to describe the extent to which a firm's stakeholder groups differ in their political preferences. Using novel large-scale data---596 million point-of-interest-month observations from mobile location data and 45 million individual employment records linked to voter registration files---I develop measures of political divergence between two important stakeholder groups (customers and employees) for millions of U.S. business locations. Even accounting for differences in geography, I document substantial variation: many businesses have a consistent partisan skew, while others serve politically diverse coalitions. I then examine the consequences of pluralism by testing whether firms with greater cross-stakeholder political divergence suffer from lower organizational performance, particularly during periods of elevated partisan conflict. By introducing stakeholder political pluralism as a dimension of stakeholder management, this paper extends stakeholder theory to account for ideological heterogeneity and provides practical implications for firms operating in divided societies.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Stakeholder theory, political ideology, consumer behavior, employee ideology, corporate political activity
\end{abstract}
